\title{A High‑Performance Rule‑Based Multi‑Agent Framework for Spatio‑Temporal Vector Dynamics: Application to \textit{Anopheles stephensi} in Dire Dawa, Ethiopia}

\date{}

\maketitle

\section*{Abstract}
\textbf{Background}: Vector‑borne diseases (VBDs) remain a major public health challenge in Africa, exacerbated by rapid urbanisation and the spread of invasive vectors such as \textit{Anopheles stephensi}. Data scarcity in resource‑limited settings hinders traditional modelling approaches. \textbf{Methods}: We present a novel, high‑performance rule‑based Multi‑Agent Simulation (MAS) framework that integrates heterogeneous environmental layers (temperature, precipitation, building density, population) with a temperature‑dependent Metzler matrix life‑cycle model. The architecture uses a concurrent spatial registry with StampedLock optimisation and an object‑pooling lifecycle manager to simulate large agent populations on standard hardware. We validate the framework using a 60‑day simulation covering the entire Dire Dawa woreda (≈1200 km²) and present results from a full‑year simulation (2025). \textbf{Results}: The 60‑day simulation successfully managed up to 250,000 active agents, maintaining an average tick time of 26.75 ms and evaluating 1.27 million agent‑rule interactions per second. Biological outputs revealed a distinct population “tipping point” as mean temperatures exceeded 30 °C, with only 2.2 \% of the initial population surviving. Surviving adults remained clustered within ∼45 m of water tanks, demonstrating infrastructure‑led persistence. The observed population growth rate correlated well with the theoretical maximum eigenvalue of the Metzler matrix ($r$ = 0.87). The full‑year simulation (35,040 ticks) showed two distinct seasonal peaks corresponding to the bimodal rainfall pattern, with a dry‑season trough in February (adult abundance <500) and wet‑season peaks in April (∼18,000) and August (∼22,000). \textbf{Conclusion}: The framework provides a scalable, biologically grounded synthetic data generator for vector surveillance in data‑scarce urban environments. The full‑year simulation identifies optimal windows for larviciding and supports scenario testing of interventions.

\paragraph{Keywords:} multi‑agent systems, vector‑borne disease, \textit{Anopheles stephensi}, Metzler matrix, high‑performance computing, spatial ecology.

\section{Introduction}

Vector‑borne diseases (VBDs) account for approximately 700,000 deaths annually, with malaria alone threatening nearly half the world's population \citep{2025.RL.HMD}. Sub‑Saharan Africa bears 94 \% of the global malaria burden \citep{2024.WH.WWM}. The recent invasion of \textit{Anopheles stephensi} into urban Africa poses an unprecedented threat. Unlike endemic African vectors, \textit{An. stephensi} thrives in artificial containers and man‑made structures, making it well‑adapted to rapidly growing cities \citep{2025.GC.SAB}. Its establishment in Djibouti led to a 30‑fold increase in malaria cases within seven years \citep{2022.AH.TPI}. In Ethiopia, the vector has been detected in several cities including Dire Dawa, raising urgent questions about its future spread and control.

A major bottleneck is the pervasive “data gap” in resource‑constrained settings: high‑quality entomological and environmental data are often sparse, limiting the applicability of conventional statistical and compartmental models. Agent‑based models (ABMs) offer a promising alternative by simulating individual behaviours and local interactions, but general‑purpose platforms (e.g., NetLogo, GAMA) struggle with city‑scale simulations due to performance constraints \citep{2021.DG.DMI,2024.RR.COE}. Custom high‑performance ABMs can overcome these limitations by leveraging concurrent data structures, spatial indexing, and optimised lifecycle management.

In this paper, we present a novel MAS framework designed for large‑scale vector simulations. We demonstrate its utility by modelling \textit{An. stephensi} dynamics across the entire Dire Dawa woreda (≈1200 km²) for a 60‑day period and extend the simulation to a full year (2025). Our contributions are threefold: (1) a high‑performance ABM architecture with a concurrent spatial registry and object pooling; (2) integration of a temperature‑dependent Metzler matrix life‑cycle model; (3) validation using field‑derived parameters and spatial clustering analysis, plus seasonal forecasts from a full‑year run.

\section{Materials and Methods}

\subsection{Study Area}
Dire Dawa (9.604°N, 41.856°E) is the second largest city in Ethiopia, with a population exceeding 500,000. The woreda covers approximately 1200 km², encompassing urban, peri‑urban, and rural areas. The climate is semi‑arid, with a bimodal rainfall pattern (short rains: March–April; long rains: July–September) and mean annual temperature of ∼24 °C. \textit{An. stephensi} has been confirmed in the area through entomological surveys \citep{2022.AA.VCS}.

\subsubsection{Spatial Registry}
The `SpatialRegistry` implements a grid‑based spatial indexing system with a cell size of 0.001° (∼111 m). It uses `StampedLock` optimistic read locking to allow concurrent queries during agent rule evaluation while maintaining consistency for updates. Batch updates (births, deaths, movements) are processed asynchronously, reducing synchronisation overhead by up to 90 \%. The registry maintains a cache of frequently accessed cells (LRU, max 1000 entries), achieving a cache hit rate of 73 \% in our simulations.

\subsubsection{Agent Classes}
Two agent classes are defined:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{LivingAgent}: Mobile agents representing mosquitoes. They maintain state variables (stage, age, energy, gravidity, resting status) using atomic types for thread safety.
    \item \texttt{InertAgent}: Stationary agents representing water containers (breeding sites). They track water volume, egg count, larval count, and carrying capacity.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Rule Engine}
The `RuleEngine` uses GraalVM JavaScript contexts to evaluate behavioural rules (Table \ref{tab:agent_rules}). Rules are defined in YAML configuration and prioritised (1–10). The engine caches compiled conditions, reducing parsing overhead by ∼95 \%. Actions include feeding, resting, oviposition, and mortality, with critical actions automatically invalidating spatial caches.

\subsection{Metzler Matrix Life‑Cycle Model}
The temperature‑dependent development and survival are modelled using a four‑stage Metzler matrix (eggs, larvae, pupae, adults). The population vector $\mathbf{N}(t) = [E, L, P, A]^T$ evolves as:

\begin{equation}
\frac{d\mathbf{N}}{dt} = \mathbf{M}(T) \mathbf{N},
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{M}(T)$ is the transition matrix:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{M}(T) = \begin{pmatrix}
-d_E(T) & 0 & 0 & F(T) \cdot K(L) \\
S_E(T) d_E(T) & -d_L(T) & 0 & 0 \\
0 & S_L(T) d_L(T) & -d_P(T) & 0 \\
0 & 0 & S_P(T) d_P(T) & -\mu_A(T)
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}

The definitions of the rates and survival probabilities are given in Table \ref{tab:model_params}. The per‑tick probabilities for stage transitions are computed as $p = 1 - \exp(-r \cdot \Delta t)$, where $\Delta t$ is the tick duration (0.0104167 days for 15‑minute ticks). Adult mortality is applied similarly.

The host carrying capacity $K(L) = 1 + \log(1 + L)$ scales fecundity with local livestock density $L$ (persons per km²). This formulation accounts for the availability of blood hosts and the density‑dependent reduction in oviposition success when hosts are scarce.

\subsection{Simulation Setup}
\subsubsection{Data Integration}
Environmental layers (elevation, building density, population density) were loaded from GeoTIFF files at 100 m resolution. Climate data (2 m temperature, total precipitation) were extracted from ERA5‑Land reanalysis at 0.1° hourly resolution for the period January–December 2025. The spatial domain covers the entire Dire Dawa woreda (bounding box: 41.73°E–42.34°E, 9.47°N–9.78°N). The study area shapefile was used to constrain agent seeding and visualisation.

\subsubsection{Initialisation}
We seeded 10,000 water tanks (InertAgent) and 250,000 mosquitoes (LivingAgent) using a weighted random placement algorithm favouring areas with building density >0.15 and population density >0.05 (tanks) or >0.02 (mosquitoes). The initial stage distribution of mosquitoes was 60 \% adults, 25 \% larvae, 10 \% pupae, 5 \% eggs.

\subsubsection{Execution}
A 60‑day simulation (September–October 2025, 5,760 ticks) was run as a proof‑of‑concept. Subsequently, a full‑year simulation (January–December 2025, 35,040 ticks) was executed on the same hardware (2‑core laptop, Intel Core i5‑6300U, 8 GB RAM). Output snapshots were exported every 100 ticks and later merged for analysis.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Population Dynamics – 60‑day Simulation}
The 60‑day simulation processed 340,126 mosquito life‑cycles. Peak adult abundance (249,402) occurred on day 1.04. A sharp decline commenced as the modelled 2 m temperature exceeded 30 °C, reaching a maximum of 37.1 °C on day 8.3. By day 26, the population stabilised at 5,487 adults (2.2 \% of peak). The observed daily log‑growth rate correlated well with the theoretical maximum eigenvalue $\lambda_{\max}$ of the Metzler matrix (Pearson’s $r=0.87$, $p<0.001$; Figure \ref{fig:growth}).

\subsection{Spatial Clustering and Proximity Analysis}
Adult mosquitoes remained strongly clustered around water tanks throughout the simulation. The mean distance to the nearest tank was 45.3 m (SD = 12.7 m), with 95 \% of adults within 70 m (Figure \ref{fig:proximity}). Hotspot analysis (local Moran’s I) identified persistent high‑density clusters in areas with high building and population density, consistent with the vector’s urban adaptation.

\subsection{Full‑Year Simulation Results}
The full‑year simulation (35,040 ticks) captured the seasonal dynamics of \textit{An. stephensi} in Dire Dawa (Figure \ref{fig:yearly}). Two distinct peaks in adult abundance were observed:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A minor peak in April (∼18,000 adults) following the short rains.
    \item A major peak in August (∼22,000 adults) after the long rains.
    \item A pronounced trough in February (adult abundance <500) during the dry season.
\end{itemize}
The population exhibited strong temperature and precipitation sensitivity: adult abundance increased sharply when weekly mean temperature exceeded 25 °C and rainfall >10 mm. The dry‑season trough corresponds to water tank desiccation (mean water volume <5 \%), while wet‑season peaks coincide with container refilling and high humidity.

\subsection{Performance Metrics}
The framework maintained an average tick time of 26.75 ms (60‑day) and 31.2 ms (full‑year, due to additional data loading). Peak memory usage was 5.5 GB. The spatial registry handled 1.2 million queries per second with a cache hit rate of 73 \%. These results demonstrate that year‑long city‑scale simulations are feasible on standard hardware, enabling scenario testing without HPC resources.

\section{Discussion}

The MAS framework successfully reproduces key ecological patterns of \textit{An. stephensi}: temperature‑driven population collapse, spatial clustering around water infrastructure, and strong alignment with the theoretical Metzler matrix dynamics. The high correlation between observed growth rates and $\lambda_{\max}$ validates the integration of the temperature‑dependent life‑cycle model into the agent‑based setting.

The full‑year simulation provides actionable insights for vector control. The bimodal peaks suggest that larviciding campaigns should be intensified in March–April and July–August, targeting water tanks before the onset of the rains. The dry‑season trough (February) could be exploited for source reduction (elimination of unused containers) with minimal impact on non‑target organisms. Early warning thresholds can be derived from the simulation: three consecutive days with temperature >25 °C and cumulative rainfall >15 mm precede a population surge by 2–3 weeks.

The spatial clustering results have practical implications. Targeting water tanks within a 50 m radius of human dwellings could maximise impact while minimising resources – a “precision larviciding” strategy that is both efficient and feasible in resource‑limited settings. The observed population collapse above 30 °C suggests that high‑altitude cities (e.g., Addis Ababa) may remain at lower risk unless the vector adapts to cooler temperatures, but the warm wet season could still sustain transmission in lowland areas.

A limitation of the current study is the absence of concurrent field entomological data for Dire Dawa. However, the model parameters are derived from peer‑reviewed literature on \textit{An. stephensi} thermal biology \citep{2022.VE.ETB,2025.CG.EOT}, and the spatial patterns are consistent with known ecology. Future work will incorporate local trap data as they become available, and we will use Approximate Bayesian Computation to refine parameters. We also plan to simulate indoor residual spraying (IRS) and larviciding scenarios to evaluate intervention effectiveness.

\section{Conclusion}
We have developed a high‑performance, rule‑based MAS framework that integrates a temperature‑dependent Metzler matrix life‑cycle model and a concurrent spatial registry. The framework successfully simulated 250,000 mosquitoes across the entire Dire Dawa woreda for 60 days and for a full year, revealing temperature‑driven population crashes, infrastructure‑led spatial persistence, and seasonal peaks in April and August. The full‑year simulation identifies optimal windows for vector control and provides a baseline for scenario testing. The architecture is open‑source and designed for easy adaptation to other vector species and geographic regions.
