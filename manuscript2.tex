\section*{abstract}
\textbf{Background}: Vector-borne diseases (VBDs) remain a significant public health challenge in Africa, 
exacerbated by severe data gaps in rapidly urbanizing regions. Traditional statistical models often lack 
the granularity to capture the complex interplay between invasive vectors, such as Anopheles stephensi, 
and urban micro-climates. \textbf{Methods}: We present a novel, high-performance rule-based Multi-Agent 
Simulation (MAS) framework designed to synthesize spatio-temporal vector dynamics. The architecture utilizes 
a concurrent spatial registry with StampedLock optimization and an object-pooling lifecycle manager to ensure 
scalability on standard hardware. We validated the framework using a 60-day case study across a 5 km radius of 
Dire Dawa, Ethiopia, integrating heterogeneous GIS layers including elevation, temperature, and 10000 urban 
water infrastructure points. \textbf{Results}: The simulation successfully managed a peak population of 250,000 
active agents, maintaining a high computational throughput of 1.27 million agent-rule evaluations per second with 
an average tick time of 26.75 ms. Biological outputs revealed a distinct population "tipping point" as mean 
temperatures exceeded 30°C, with a final survival of 2.2\% (5,487 agents). Surviving agents exhibited highly 
significant spatial clustering, maintaining a consistent mean proximity of ~45 meters to water infrastructure,
 validating the model's ability to simulate urban micro-climate. Conclusion: This framework provides a robust, 
 scalable tool for generating biologically grounded synthetic data. By identifying environmental "tipping points" 
 and infrastructure-led persistence, it offers a viable decision-support solution for vector surveillance in 
 resource-constrained and data-scarce urban environments.

\paragraph{Keywords:} mutli-agent systems, vector-borne disease, high performance computing, vector  surveillance, vector control.

\section{Introduction}
\par
Vector-borne diseases (VBDs) represent one of the most profound challenges to global health security, accounting for 
approximately 700,000 deaths annually and imposing significant economic burdens on developing nations \citep{2025.RL.HMD}. 
Malaria alone threatens nearly half the world's population, with Sub-Saharan Africa bearing 94\% of the global case burden 
as \citep{2024.WH.WWM}. While the past two decades witnessed substantial reductions in malaria mortality through coordinated 
interventions, this hard-won progress faces unprecedented threats from ecological and demographic shifts. 
The rapid urbanization of Sub-Saharan Africa, currently the world's fastest urbanizing region \citep{2019.IS.UAI}, 
has created novel transmission ecologies that challenge historical assumptions about vector ecology and control 
strategies \citep{2024.NK.MBD}.
\par
The primary driver of this epidemiological transition is the globalization of previously localized vectors, particularly 
the invasive malaria mosquito \textit{Anopheles stephensi}. Unlike endemic African malaria vectors that predominantly 
breed in natural water bodies, \textit{An. stephensi} exhibits remarkable ecological plasticity, thriving in artificial 
containers and man-made structures characteristic of urban environments \citep{2025.GC.SAB}. This species shares critical 
adaptive traits with \textit{Aedes aegypti}, the primary dengue vector, including container breeding, anthropophily, and 
exophilic resting behaviors \citep{2022.AH.TPI}. The consequences of this adaptation were discored in Djibouti, 
where\textit{ An. stephensi} establishment triggered a 30-fold increase in malaria cases within seven years, transforming a 
nation on the verge of malaria elimination into a high-transmission zone \citep{2022.AH.TPI}. This vector's capacity to exploit 
previously low-risk urban settings necessitates fundamentally new approaches to surveillance, modeling, and 
intervention design \citep{2024.TM.AQA}.
\par
A critical bottleneck in responding to this emerging threat is the pervasive "Data Gap" characterizing many resource-constrained 
settings where \textit{An. stephensi} is establishing. Effective vector control relies on robust decision-support systems built 
upon high-quality, high-resolution entomological and epidemiological data, yet national surveillance programs often operate with 
limited financial and technological resources \citep{2025.RL.HMD}. Current modeling approaches struggle to bridge the gap between 
theoretical elegance and practical utility: empirical models propagate measurement errors through unreliable data streams, while 
process-based models frequently lack the spatial granularity and computational efficiency needed for operational 
deployment \citep{2025.RL.HMD}. This disconnect is particularly acute for invasive vectors, where absence of historical 
baseline data undermines traditional statistical approaches \citep{2019.EL.ALO}.
\par
To address these challenges, we propose a novel computational framework that integrates high-performance computing methodologies 
with ecological process modeling. Our system implements a layered, rule-based Multi-Agent Simulation (MAS) architecture that 
harmonizes heterogeneous spatio-temporal data streams into a unified analytical environment. This framework is part of the 
family of ABS (Agent Based Simulation) which are simulation models where the researcher explicitly describe the decision 
processes of simulated actors at micro-level \citep{2008.PE.ITM}. The proposed framework bridges Computer Science and 
Environmental Science by conceptualizing environmental data as dynamic information layers, including static rasters (for instance: 
elevation, building density, population density), time-series climate variables, and vector data (shapefiles), that interact with 
autonomous agent populations. We distinguish between two agent classes: "Inert Agents" representing static environmental features 
(urban water containers serving as breeding sites) and "Living Agents" modeling biological vectors (An. stephensi mosquitoes for 
example) with behavioral rules derived from empirical studies.
\par
This study demonstrates how such synthetic modeling approaches can provide critical insights where field data are scarce or 
unreliable. Using Dire Dawa, an Ethiopian city experiencing \textit{An. stephensi} invasion, as a proof-of-concept, we simulate 
60 days of mosquito population dynamics across a 5 km radius from city's centroid. Our results illustrate how agent-based modeling 
can identify high-risk micro-habitats, simulate seasonal population trajectories, and potentially assist intervention strategies 
in data-limited settings. By making the simulation framework open-source and computationally efficient, we aim to empower public 
health agencies in resource-constrained regions with decision-support tools capable of addressing the unique challenges posed 
by urban-adapted invasive vectors.


\section{Literature review}

\subsection{Limitations of traditional epidemiological models in urban contexts}
\par
Conventional compartmental models, notably the SIR (Susceptible-Infectious-Recovered) and SEIR (Susceptible-Exposed-Infectious-Recovered) 
frameworks have formed the backbone of infectious disease modeling for decades \citep{1927.WI.ACO}. These models excel in population-level 
analysis under the critical assumption of homogeneous mixing, where transmission probability depends solely on the proportions of 
susceptible and infectious individuals. However, this fundamental assumption becomes problematic when applied to urban malaria transmission 
dynamics, particularly for container-breeding vectors like \textit{Anopheles stephensi}. Urban environments exhibit pronounced spatial 
heterogeneity in both vector breeding sites (limited to artificial water containers) and human populations (varying building densities 
and socioeconomic factors), creating transmission hotspots that compartmental models cannot capture \citep{2020.SM.TIO}. Furthermore, 
traditional models lack the spatial granularity necessary for targeted interventions, which require identification of specific breeding 
site clusters rather than uniform area-wide approaches \citep{2016.ST.OTI}. These limitations become particularly acute when modeling 
invasive species adapting to novel urban ecologies, where transmission dynamics deviate substantially from rural patterns \citep{2020.MS.ANM}.

\subsection{Agent-Based Modeling in Ecological Applications: Capabilities and Constraints}
\par 
ABM (Agent-Based Modeling) emerged as a powerful alternative to compartmental models by representing individual entities (agents) with 
autonomous behaviors and localized interactions \citep{2006.VO.ASP}. In vector ecology, platforms like NetLogo and GAMA have enabled 
researchers to simulate mosquito behaviors, life cycles, and host-seeking patterns \citep{2010.PA.GAS,2013.AD.GMA,2024.RR.COE}. These 
tools excel at modeling small-scale ecological processes such as oviposition site selection or sugar feeding behavior, hrough intuitive 
visual programming interfaces and extensive libraries of pre-built ecological functions \citep{2020.SF.BLF}. However, their architecture 
encounters significant limitations when scaling to city-wide simulations with millions of agents and complex environmental datasets 
\citep{2021.DG.DMI}. Performance constraints arise from interpretive execution (NetLogo) and limited parallelization capabilities, 
typically restricting simulations to tens of thousands of agents before computational overhead becomes prohibitive 
\citep{2024.RR.COE,2024.CG.LLM}. More critically, these platforms exhibit limited capacity for deep integration with multi-layered 
GIS (Geographic Information System) rasters at native resolutions, a crucial requirement for urban vector modeling where building 
footprints, elevation, and population density operate at different spatial scales and require simultaneous querying during agent 
decision-making. The "drag-and-drop" paradigm, while accessible, sacrifices computational efficiency and customizability, 
particularly for memory-intensive operations like temporal interpolation of climate data across thousands of simulation ticks 
\citep{2021.DG.DMI, 2013.AD.GMA}. These constraints necessitate custom solutions when modeling regional-scale vector populations 
with high spatiotemporal resolution.

\subsection{High-Performance Computing Approaches in Multi-Agent Systems}
\par
Recent advances in MAS (Multi-Agent Systems) for ecological applications have increasingly leveraged High-Performance Computing 
(HPC) architectures to overcome scalability limitations. Contemporary frameworks emphasize concurrent data structures and spatial 
indexing algorithms that enable efficient simulation of millions of interacting agents across distributed computing environments. 
The spatial indexing challenge, rapidly identifying neighbors within agent perception raduis, has driven innovation in grid-based 
partitioning, quad-trees, and R-tree implementations optimized for parallel access patterns \citep{2021.DG.DMI}. Thread-safety 
mechanisms, particularly Java's StampedLock with optimistic read patterns, have demonstrated order-of-magnitude improvements over 
traditional synchronization methods by allowing concurrent read operations during intensive spatial queries while maintaining data 
consistency for position updates. These technical considerations justify the development of custom simulation engines over pre-packaged 
solutions: while commercial and open-source platforms provide rapid prototyping capabilities, they often lack the low-level control 
required for memory-mapped raster access, lock-free concurrent data structures, and cache-aware spatial indexing necessary for 
large-scale simulations. The present framework addresses these gaps through a Java-based architecture that balances object-oriented 
design with HPC optimizations, enabling simulation of $ \geq $ 500,000 agents with integrated environmental layers while 
maintaining sub-second tick times; performance characteristics unattainable with general-purpose ABM platforms \citep{2024.RR.COE}.

\section{Materials \& Methods}
\par
We developed a novel, high-performance agent-based modeling framework to simulate urban mosquito population dynamics in urban 
environments. The system integrates heterogeneous environmental datasets with behavioral rules to model mosquito life cycles, 
breeding site utilization, and human-vector interactions at fine spatiotemporal resolutions.

\subsection{Framework Architecture and Computational Design}
\par 
The Multi-Agent Simulation (MAS) system was implemented as an object-oriented Java framework designed specifically for the computational 
demands of regional-scale vector modeling. The framework's architecture was built around several core design principles: modularity 
for integrating diverse data sources, performance optimization for handling large agent populations, and flexibility for adapting to 
different mosquito species and environmental contexts (see Figure \ref{fig:arch}).

\subsubsection{Layered Information Model}
\par 
The environmental representation follows a stacked-layer paradigm where heterogeneous data sources are integrated into a unified spatial 
framework. Static environmental variables are managed through specialized raster layer management classes. These classes implement efficient 
coordinate-to-value mapping using georeferenced bounding boxes and bilinear interpolation, enabling agents to query localized environmental 
conditions during simulation runtime with minimal computational overhead. Specifically, for memory-intensive datasets exceeding 100 mega 
bites, we implemented MemoryMappedRasterLayer class which utilizes Java's NIO (New Input/Output) memory-mapped file channels, allowing 
direct access to disk-based raster data without loading entire datasets into heap memory. This approach reduces memory footprint by 
approximately 70\% for large-scale simulations while maintaining sub-millisecond access times for environmental queries.
\par
Spatiotemporal climate variables in NetCDF(Network Common Data Form) require specialized handling due to their time-varying nature. 
The InterpolatedRasterLayer class implements a three-dimensional data structure [time][latitude][longitude] with linear temporal 
interpolation between hourly climate reanalysis timesteps. The class maintains an in-memory cache of interpolated values for the 
current simulation time, using a ReentrantReadWriteLock synchronization mechanism that allows concurrent read access by thousands 
of agents while ensuring thread-safe updates during temporal transitions. This design can for instance enable smooth diurnal temperature 
cycles and precipitation events to influence agent behavior without computational bottlenecks.

\subsubsection{Agent Representation and Management}
\par
The framework distinguishes between two fundamental agent classes based on mobility and autonomy.  LivingAgent class in designed for 
entities with comprehensive internal state management, in our case mosquitoes, including: (1) developmental stage (egg, larva, pupa, adult) 
with stage-specific physiological parameters, (2) energy reserves (0.0-1.0 scale) depleted by activity and replenished through feeding, (3) 
age (in 15-minute simulation ticks) tracking cumulative exposure, (4) gravidity status indicating reproductive readiness, and (5) behavioral 
state (resting, active, seeking). These state variables are implemented using atomic data types (AtomicBoolean, AtomicInteger, AtomicReference) 
to ensure thread-safe concurrent modification during parallel rule evaluation.
\par
The InertAgent class is designed for stationary entities that maintain an evolving internal state throughout the simulation. These agents 
represent static environmental features (such as water containers or breeding sites) that interact dynamically with living agents at runtime. 
Although they lack autonomous movement, they remain active participants in the ecosystem's logic. Agent organization follows a hierarchical 
structure where agents are grouped into layers managed by the AgentLayer class. Each agent layer hosts a homogeneous population of either 
living or inert agents. This separation allows for differentiated rule sets and update frequencies optimized for each agent type. The 
AgentContainer class within each layer implements a partitioned storage system that distributes agents across multiple thread-safe queues, 
enabling parallel processing during simulation ticks with minimal inter-thread contention.

\subsubsection{Performance Optimization}
\par 
Simulating urban-scale mosquito populations ($ \geq $ 500,000 agents) necessitated several computational optimizations. To this end, 
we design a SpatialRegistry class that implements a grid-based spatial indexing system with 0.001-degree resolution (~111-meter cells),
 providing O(1) access time for agent position updates and neighbor queries. We employed Java's StampedLock synchronization primitive 
 which supports optimistic read locking, allowing concurrent read operations during the intensive neighbor-finding phase of agent rule 
 evaluation while maintaining data consistency for position updates.
\par
To address the high object turnover inherent in mosquito population dynamics (daily birth/death rates ~10\%), we implemented an 
object pooling system through the AgentLifecycleManager class. This manager maintains recyclable agent instances in size-limited 
pools (maximum 10,000 agents per type), significantly reducing garbage collection pauses by reinitializing rather than reallocating 
agent objects. During simulation execution, birth events draw from these pools (cache hit ~85\%), while deceased agents are returned 
for future reuse rather than being garbage collected.
\par
Batch processing further enhances performance by aggregating lifecycle events (births, deaths, movements) into bulk operations. The 
system accumulates individual events throughout the rule evaluation phase, then processes them collectively using optimized bulk 
operations in the SpatialRegistry, reducing synchronization overhead by up to 90\% compared to individual event processing.

\subsection{Rule-Based Logic and Decision Engine}
\par 
Agent decision-making follows a modular rule-based paradigm implemented in the RuleEngine class. Each rule consists of three 
components: (1) a condition expressed in JavaScript syntax, (2) an action identifier from a predefined set of behavioral primitives, 
and (3) a priority integer determining evaluation order when multiple conditions are satisfied. The rule engine utilizes the GraalVM 
polyglot runtime to evaluate conditions within isolated JavaScript contexts, providing sandboxed execution while maintaining high 
performance (~100,000 rule evaluations/second/core).
\par
Rules are parameterized through a token system that maps environmental layer names to accessible variables within condition expressions. 
For example, a temperature raster layer named "t2m" can be accessed via the token "temperature" in rule conditions. This abstraction layer 
decouples rule logic from underlying data structures, allowing easy rule modifications and human readability. The engine maintains a 
thread-local cache of compiled rule conditions, reducing parsing overhead for frequently evaluated rules by approximately 95\%.
\par
The action repertoire includes biologically grounded behaviors:  "die" (agent mortality), "get\_gravid" (reproductive maturation), 
"lay\_eggs" (oviposition in suitable containers), "hatch" (egg-to-larva transition), "feed" (energy acquisition), "move\_random" (dispersal), 
"reproduce" (sexual reproduction), "pupate" (larva-to-pupa metamorphosis), "emerge" (pupa-to-adult eclosion), "dry\_out" (container desiccation), 
"freeze" (cold-induced mortality), "evaporate" (water loss), "rest"/"stop\_resting" (behavioral state transitions), and "rest\_in\_building" 
(anthropogenic shelter seeking). Each action modifies agent state variables and/or triggers spatial registry updates, with critical actions 
(e.g., "die", "lay\_eggs") automatically invalidating spatial caches to maintain data consistency.
\par
Rule priorities range from 1 (lowest) to 10 (highest), with higher-priority rules evaluated first. This hierarchy ensures that critical 
behaviors (e.g., mortality from extreme temperatures at priority 10) supersede less urgent actions (e.g., movement at priority 2). Within 
each priority level, rules are evaluated sequentially until a terminal action (e.g. "die") executes, at which point further rule evaluation 
ceases for that agent during the current tick, optimizing computational resources.

\subsection{Case Study Design: Anopheles stephensi in Dire Dawa, Ethiopia}
\subsubsection{Study Area and Justification}
\par 
We selected Dire Dawa, Ethiopia (9.604°N, 41.856°E) as our validation case study due to several factors: (1) confirmed presence of invasive 
Anopheles stephensi based on recent entomological surveys (Balkew et al., 2020; Balkew et al., 2023; Ahmed et al., 2022), (2) representative 
East African urban ecology with mixed formal/informal settlements, (3) available high-resolution environmental datasets, and (4) epidemiological 
significance as an emerging malaria transmission zone. Anopheles stephensi presents particular modeling challenges due to its behavioral 
plasticity; unlike most African malaria vectors, it thrives in urban environments by exploiting anthropogenic water containers for breeding 
and exhibits both endophilic (indoor-resting) and exophilic (outdoor-resting) behaviors \citep{2020.MS.ANM}.
\par
The simulation domain encompassed a 5-kilometer radius circular buffer ($ \approx $ 78.5 $km^2$) centered on Dire Dawa's urban city/town. 
This spatial extent captures the typical flight range of Anopheles stephensi (usually <1 km \citep{1966.MA.FRL}) while including peri-urban 
transition zones where breeding site availability changes dramatically. The buffer approach also accommodated spatial uncertainty in agent 
initialization and prevented edge effects from influencing population dynamics.

\subsubsection{Data Integration}
\par 
Environmental data integration followed a multi-source, multi-resolution approach. Elevation data (30m resolution) originated from 
NASA's Shuttle Radar Topography Mission (SRTM), resampled to 100m resolution using cubic convolution to balance detail and computational 
load. This data was easily accessed via Google Earth Engine API. Building footprints were derived from Google Earth Engine building 
footprint dataset, with density calculated using 100m grid cells to represent urbanization gradients. Population distribution utilized 
WorldPop's 100m resolution constrained-adjusted estimates for 2020, providing realistic human host availability.
\par
Historical climate data timeseries were derived from ERA5-Land reanalysis data at 0.1 degree spatial resolution and hourly temporal 
resolution. We extracted a 4-month period (September-December 2025) encompassing the post-rainy season when Anopheles stephensi populations 
typically peak \citep{2023.AK.DAA}. The dataset included 2m temperature (K), total precipitation (m), and derived variables including 
relative humidity and dew point temperature. These hourly values were temporally interpolated to 15-minute simulation ticks using the 
InterpolatedRasterLayer class linear interpolation, capturing diurnal temperature fluctuations critical for mosquito physiology.
\par
Validation data comprised Anopheles stephensi occurrence records from the Global Biodiversity Information Facility (GBIF), filtered 
for quality (coordinate uncertainty $<$1 km) and temporal relevance. These presence-only records provided spatial benchmarks for comparing 
simulated population distributions against empirical observations.

\subsubsection{Simulation Parameters}
\par 
A 60-day simulation (September 1-October 30, 2025) was initialized with parameter values derived from published Anopheles stephensi 
ecology (Tables \ref{tab:agent_rules} \& \ref{tab:model_parameters}). Living agents were spatially distributed using a weighted 
random placement algorithm favoring areas with: (1) building density $>$20\%, (2) population density $>$ 50 persons/$km^{2}$, 
and (3) elevation between 1,000-1,500m. This produced an initial population of 500,000 mosquitoes with stage distribution: 60\% 
adults, 25\% larvae, 10\% pupae, 5\% eggs, reflecting typical age structure during seasonal peaks \citep{2020.MS.ANM,2020.MB.GDO, 2021.FG.ASM}.
\par 
Inert agents (water containers) were distributed according to a Poisson point process with intensity varying by building density: 
high-density urban areas ($\geq 40\%$) building coverage) received 4 containers/100 $m^2$, medium-density (20--40\%) received 2 
containers/100 $m^2$, and low-density ($< 20\%$) received 1 container/100 $m^2$. Each container initialized with randomized attributes: 
water volume (30--100\% capacity), egg count (0--50), larval count (0--20), and capacity (100--500 individuals) representing heterogeneous 
container types from small jars to large water tanks.

\subsubsection{Output and Analysis}
\par
The simulation exported comprehensive state snapshots at 100-tick intervals (25 hours real-time), each containing: (1) georeferenced 
agent positions with complete state vectors, (2) environmental conditions (temperature, precipitation, building density) at each agent's 
location, (3) container status (water volume, egg/larval counts), and (4) system performance metrics (tick duration, memory usage, agent counts). 
These CSV-formatted snapshots were stored in CSV (comma separated values) files and later merged into a single file for analysis and visualization.
\par
Post-simulation analysis focused on three validation metrics: (1) spatial correlation between simulated adult density and GBIF occurrence records 
using Ripley's K-function, (2) temporal population trajectory comparison against published seasonal abundance curves for Anopheles stephensi, 
and (3) container utilization rates compared to larval survey data from similar urban settings. Additionally, we performed sensitivity analysis 
on key parameters (Table 2 uncertainty ranges) using Latin Hypercube sampling to identify drivers of population dynamics.
\par
The computational implementation achieved a sustained performance of approximately 2.5 simulation days per hour of compute time on a 16-core 
workstation (AMD Ryzen 9 5950X, 64GB RAM), with memory usage stabilizing at 8-12GB after initialization. This performance envelope enables 
multi-year simulations at urban scales, supporting both research applications and scenario testing for vector control interventions.

\subsection{Spatio-Temporal Population Dynamics}
\par
The framework successfully synthesized the complex lifecycle dynamics of 340,126 total mosquito agents over a 60-day period. 
Initial population states (Day 1.04) showed a robust distribution: 152,081 Adults (61\%), 60,768 Larvae (24\%), and 36,553 Pupae (15\%).
\par
Unlike smaller cohorts, this high-density population exhibited a distinct "resilience phase" before environmental attrition. 
A precipitous decline was observed as the modeled 2m temperature rose from 26.1°C to a peak of 37.1°C by Day 8.3. By Day 26, 
the population had stabilized at 5,487 agents, representing a survival rate of 2.2\% of the peak population. This collapse 
confirms the model's sensitivity to cumulative thermal stress, accurately reflecting the biological limits of the species.

\subsection{Urban Micro-refugia and Spatial Clustering}
\par
Spatial analysis revealed that the vector-infrastructure interface is the primary determinant of persistence. Using k-d 
tree proximity analysis, the mean distance of adult mosquitoes to the nearest water tank remained remarkably constant 
between 44.5 and 46.2 meters, despite the massive population flux. As environmental temperatures increased, the spatial 
density contracted into "hotspots" directly over water tank coordinates. This confirms that \textit{An. stephensi} can 
persist in arid urban environments provided that human-made water storage infrastructure is available within a 50-meter 
flight radius.

\subsection{Thermal Constraints and Resource Scarcity}
\par
The simulation statistics reveal the intense computational and biological interaction between the vector agents and the 
urban landscape. The final statistics indicated a total "rules evaluated" count of 58,830 per tick for the water tank layer. 
This high volume of interaction suggests that despite the vast urban space, mosquito agents consistently localized and 
interacted with the 9,805 identified resource points.
\par
The absence of active mosquito agents in the final simulation snapshot (Tick 5760) points to a complete population collapse 
driven by the environmental "tipping point." This collapse was precipitated by a sustained thermal breach; the modeled 2m 
temperature exceeded 35.0°C mid-simulation (reaching a peak of 37.1°C), which surpassed the survival threshold for all life 
stages. This result highlights the framework's utility in identifying specific environmental boundaries where climatic 
variables override the presence of favorable urban infrastructure, effectively defining the "thermal niche" of An. stephensi 
in high-altitude or arid regions.

\subsection{Computational Scalability and Framework Performance}
\par
To evaluate the framework's utility for large-scale urban surveillance, we quantified the computational overhead required to 
simulate the high-density An. stephensi population. The simulation successfully processed a total of 340,126 mosquito agent 
life-cycles alongside 9,805 static water infrastructure agents over a 60-day temporal window (5,760 ticks).
\par
Despite the high agent density, the system maintained high performance with an average tick time of 26.75 ms. The total 
runtime of 612.85 seconds (approximately 10 minutes) on standard hardware (Memory Total: 1950MB) demonstrates that this 
rule-based MAS framework is viable for deployment in resource-constrained environments where high-performance computing 
(HPC) clusters are unavailable.
\par 
The model's biological fidelity was maintained through the execution of 22 distinct behavior rules for mosquito agents and 
6 for water infrastructure. During the simulation, the framework effectively managed the transition of agents through life 
stages until environmental thresholds were reached. The "Inert" status of the water tank agents allowed for a high volume 
of spatial queries (averaging 58,830 rule evaluations per tick) without degrading system performance, facilitating a 
high-resolution analysis of the vector-resource interface.
\par
The spatial registry utilized a fixed search radius of approximately 55.7 meters, mirroring the biological flight range 
and sensory distance of Anopheles species in urban settings. This parameter ensured that mosquito-water interactions were 
localized and biologically realistic, leading to the observed clustering patterns around urban containers.
The framework optimized thread usage via a SimulationEngine with a core pool size of 3, ensuring that even at peak agent 
density (250,000+ agents), the system avoided memory leaks and maintained a stable thread-state (Uptime: 10.12 minutes).

\section{Discussion}
\par 
The findings of this study demonstrate that a layered, rule-based Multi-Agent Simulation (MAS) provides a sophisticated 
lens through which to view the invasion dynamics of Anopheles stephensi in urban African landscapes. By bridging 
high-resolution GIS data with autonomous agent logic, the framework moves beyond the limitations of traditional compartmental 
models (e.g., SIR or SEIR), which typically assume uniform mixing and spatial homogeneity. In our simulation, the emergence of 
localized "micro-habitats", where agents remained clustered around water tanks despite a regional temperature-driven population 
crash (Section 4), underscores the critical importance of micro-spatial granularity. This biological-technical synergy suggests 
that \textit{An. stephensi} does not persist as a broad regional population, but as a series of highly localized clusters tied 
to urban infrastructure. For public health authorities in cities like Addis Ababa, this implies that surveillance should not be 
uniform; instead, a "precision targeting" approach within a 50-meter radius of water storage points is likely to be more effective 
and resource-efficient.
\par
A primary contribution of this research is its ability to address the "data gap" in resource-constrained regions through the 
generation of high-fidelity synthetic data. Vector-borne disease surveillance in Africa often suffers from a lack of longitudinal 
field data \citep{2020.MS.ANM}. Our results demonstrate that even when based on fundamental ecological rules, the framework 
synthesizes population trends that align with established ecological theory, such as the high-altitude thermal barrier. The 
observation that a peak temperature of 37.1°C triggered a population collapse identifies a specific climatic "tipping point" 
for \textit{An. stephensi} in the Ethiopian highlands. For policy-makers, this synthetic output serves as a predictive baseline, 
allowing them to identify potential "hotspots" where the vector might persist during warmer seasons or following rapid changes 
in urban infrastructure. By simulating these dynamics in silico, we provide a decision-support tool that can guide field teams 
to vulnerable areas before an outbreak occurs.
\par
From a computational perspective, the performance metrics observed in Section 3.4 validate the architectural choices of Object 
Pooling and Concurrent Spatial Indexing using StampedLock optimizations. The ability to process over 1.27 million agent-rule 
evaluations per second while maintaining an average tick time of 26.75 ms for a population of 250,000+ agents is significant. 
It confirms that high-resolution modeling of entire metropolitan areas is computationally feasible on standard research hardware 
(Memory Total: 1950MB), a prerequisite for deploying such tools in regions where access to high-performance computing (HPC) 
clusters is limited. Furthermore, the decoupled nature of the Rule Engine provides "plug-and-play" adaptability. This 
architecture is future-proofed; the same engine could be pivoted to model other neglected tropical diseases (NTDs), such 
as visceral leishmaniasis, simply by swapping agent-rule definitions for sandflies, without necessitating a redesign of the 
core spatial or temporal registry.
\par
Despite these strengths, the simulation is not without limitations that warrant further empirical calibration. The rapid 
population crash observed in the results highlights the high sensitivity of our "Rule Engine" to temperature, which may be 
modulated in the real world by mosquitoes seeking refuge in indoor environments or cooler micro-climates not fully captured 
in the 2m temperature raster. Furthermore, while the current 5 km radius covers a substantial urban-peri-urban gradient, 
the anthropophilic preferences and indoor resting behaviors of \textit{An. stephensi} require more nuanced rule definitions 
to perfectly match Ethiopian field observations \citep{2021.FG.ASM}. Future work will focus on integrating real-time satellite 
climate feeds for live forecasting and implementing Genetic Algorithms to auto-calibrate rule thresholds against historical 
outbreak data. By refining these parameters, the framework will evolve from a synthetic data generator into a live, predictive 
surveillance asset for vector-borne disease management in the African context.


\section{Conclusion}
\par 
We believe this research bridges the gap between high-performance computer science and environmental epidemiology. 
By developing a custom, rule-based multi-agent architecture, we have demonstrated that it is possible to simulate complex biological 
systems at scale with high spatial and temporal fidelity. The case study of \textit{Anopheles stephensi} in Dire Dawa town in 
Ethiopia serves as a critical proof of concept. It confirms that the framework is sensitive to real-world environmental stressors 
and can be tunned to accurately model vector persistence in marginal climates. This work establishes the foundational engine required 
for larger-scale continental modeling. The modularity of the RuleEngine ensures that this system is not limited to malaria; it is a 
platform which should be capable of being tuned to other vector-borne disease such as neglected tropical diseases like visceral 
leishmaniasis. Ultimately, we believe this framework moves us closer to a "digital twin" of vector ecology, providing the 
synthetic data necessary to guide life-saving public health interventions in the world's most vulnerable regions, or regions 
with surveillance resource constraints.
