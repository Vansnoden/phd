\section*{Abstract}
\textbf{Background}: Vector-borne diseases (VBDs) remain a significant public health challenge in Africa, exacerbated by severe data gaps in rapidly urbanizing regions. Traditional statistical models often lack the granularity to capture the complex interplay between invasive vectors, such as \textit{Anopheles stephensi}, and urban micro‑climates. \textbf{Methods}: We present a novel, high-performance rule-based Multi-Agent Simulation (MAS) framework designed to synthesise spatio‑temporal vector dynamics even when field data are sparse. The architecture uses a concurrent spatial registry with StampedLock optimisation and an object‑pooling lifecycle manager to ensure scalability on standard hardware. We define a comprehensive evaluation protocol (data compilation, parameter calibration, internal/external validation, sensitivity analysis, and scenario testing) following TRACE and ODD standards. As a proof‑of‑concept, we simulate 60 days of \textit{An. stephensi} dynamics across a 5 km radius of Dire Dawa, Ethiopia, integrating heterogeneous GIS layers including elevation, temperature, and 10,000 urban water infrastructure points. \textbf{Results}: The simulation successfully managed a peak population of 250,000 active agents, maintaining a high computational throughput of 1.27 million agent‑rule evaluations per second with an average tick time of 26.75 ms. Biological outputs revealed a distinct population “tipping point” as mean temperatures exceeded 30 °C, with a final survival of 2.2 \% (5,487 agents). Surviving agents exhibited highly significant spatial clustering, maintaining a consistent mean proximity of ∼45 m to water infrastructure, validating the model’s ability to simulate urban micro‑climate effects. \textbf{Conclusion}: This framework provides a robust, scalable tool for generating biologically grounded synthetic data. By identifying environmental “tipping points” and infrastructure‑led persistence, it offers a viable decision‑support solution for vector surveillance in resource‑constrained and data‑scarce urban environments. The accompanying evaluation protocol establishes a rigorous pathway to future external validation once field data become available.

\paragraph{Keywords:} multi‑agent systems, vector‑borne disease, high performance computing, vector surveillance, vector control, \textit{Anopheles stephensi}.

\section{Introduction}

Vector‑borne diseases (VBDs) represent one of the most profound challenges to global health security, accounting for approximately 700,000 deaths annually and imposing significant economic burdens on developing nations \citep{2025.RL.HMD}. Malaria alone threatens nearly half the world's population, with Sub‑Saharan Africa bearing 94 \% of the global case burden \citep{2024.WH.WWM}. While the past two decades witnessed substantial reductions in malaria mortality through coordinated interventions, this hard‑won progress faces unprecedented threats from ecological and demographic shifts. The rapid urbanisation of Sub‑Saharan Africa, currently the world's fastest urbanising region \citep{2019.IS.UAI}, has created novel transmission ecologies that challenge historical assumptions about vector ecology and control strategies \citep{2024.NK.MBD}.

The primary driver of this epidemiological transition is the globalisation of previously localised vectors, particularly the invasive malaria mosquito \textit{Anopheles stephensi}. Unlike endemic African malaria vectors that predominantly breed in natural water bodies, \textit{An. stephensi} exhibits remarkable ecological plasticity, thriving in artificial containers and man‑made structures characteristic of urban environments \citep{2025.GC.SAB}. This species shares critical adaptive traits with \textit{Aedes aegypti}, the primary dengue vector, including container breeding, anthropophily, and exophilic resting behaviours \citep{2022.AH.TPI}. The consequences of this adaptation were observed in Djibouti, where \textit{An. stephensi} establishment triggered a 30‑fold increase in malaria cases within seven years, transforming a nation on the verge of malaria elimination into a high‑transmission zone \citep{2022.AH.TPI}. This vector's capacity to exploit previously low‑risk urban settings necessitates fundamentally new approaches to surveillance, modelling, and intervention design \citep{2024.TM.AQA}.

A critical bottleneck in responding to this emerging threat is the pervasive “data gap” characterising many resource‑constrained settings where \textit{An. stephensi} is establishing. Effective vector control relies on robust decision‑support systems built upon high‑quality, high‑resolution entomological and epidemiological data, yet national surveillance programmes often operate with limited financial and technological resources \citep{2025.RL.HMD}. Current modelling approaches struggle to bridge the gap between theoretical elegance and practical utility: empirical models propagate measurement errors through unreliable data streams, while process‑based models frequently lack the spatial granularity and computational efficiency needed for operational deployment \citep{2025.RL.HMD}. This disconnect is particularly acute for invasive vectors, where absence of historical baseline data undermines traditional statistical approaches \citep{2019.EL.ALO}.

To address these challenges, we propose a novel computational framework that integrates high‑performance computing methodologies with ecological process modelling. Our system implements a layered, rule‑based Multi‑Agent Simulation (MAS) architecture that harmonises heterogeneous spatio‑temporal data streams into a unified analytical environment. This framework is part of the family of Agent‑Based Simulations (ABS), where researchers explicitly describe the decision processes of simulated actors at the micro‑level \citep{2008.PE.ITM}. The proposed framework bridges computer science and environmental science by conceptualising environmental data as dynamic information layers—static rasters (elevation, building density, population density), time‑series climate variables, and vector data (shapefiles)—that interact with autonomous agent populations. We distinguish two agent classes: “Inert Agents” representing static environmental features (urban water containers serving as breeding sites) and “Living Agents” modelling biological vectors (e.g., \textit{An. stephensi}) with behavioural rules derived from empirical studies.

A key contribution of this work is the definition of a **rigorous evaluation protocol** that guides the use of the framework in data‑scarce environments. While the present paper reports a proof‑of‑concept simulation for Dire Dawa, Ethiopia, we also lay out a step‑by‑step plan for future calibration, validation, sensitivity analysis, and scenario testing (Section 4.5). This protocol follows established standards (TRACE and ODD) and provides placeholders for external data that are not yet available, ensuring that the framework can evolve into a fully validated decision‑support tool as new field data are collected.

\section{Literature Review}

\subsection{Limitations of Traditional Epidemiological Models in Urban Contexts}
Conventional compartmental models, notably the SIR (Susceptible‑Infectious‑Recovered) and SEIR (Susceptible‑Exposed‑Infectious‑Recovered) frameworks, have formed the backbone of infectious disease modelling for decades \citep{1927.WI.ACO}. These models excel in population‑level analysis under the critical assumption of homogeneous mixing, where transmission probability depends solely on the proportions of susceptible and infectious individuals. However, this fundamental assumption becomes problematic when applied to urban malaria transmission dynamics, particularly for container‑breeding vectors like \textit{Anopheles stephensi}. Urban environments exhibit pronounced spatial heterogeneity in both vector breeding sites (limited to artificial water containers) and human populations (varying building densities and socioeconomic factors), creating transmission hotspots that compartmental models cannot capture \citep{2020.SM.TIO}. Furthermore, traditional models lack the spatial granularity necessary for targeted interventions, which require identification of specific breeding site clusters rather than uniform area‑wide approaches \citep{2016.ST.OTI}. These limitations become particularly acute when modelling invasive species adapting to novel urban ecologies, where transmission dynamics deviate substantially from rural patterns \citep{2020.MS.ANM}.

\subsection{Agent‑Based Modelling in Ecological Applications: Capabilities and Constraints}
ABM emerged as a powerful alternative to compartmental models by representing individual entities (agents) with autonomous behaviours and localised interactions \citep{2006.VO.ASP}. In vector ecology, platforms like NetLogo and GAMA have enabled researchers to simulate mosquito behaviours, life cycles, and host‑seeking patterns \citep{2010.PA.GAS,2013.AD.GMA,2024.RR.COE}. These tools excel at modelling small‑scale ecological processes such as oviposition site selection or sugar feeding behaviour, through intuitive visual programming interfaces and extensive libraries of pre‑built ecological functions \citep{2020.SF.BLF}. However, their architecture encounters significant limitations when scaling to city‑wide simulations with millions of agents and complex environmental datasets \citep{2021.DG.DMI}. Performance constraints arise from interpretive execution (NetLogo) and limited parallelisation capabilities, typically restricting simulations to tens of thousands of agents before computational overhead becomes prohibitive \citep{2024.RR.COE,2024.CG.LLM}. More critically, these platforms exhibit limited capacity for deep integration with multi‑layered GIS rasters at native resolutions, a crucial requirement for urban vector modelling where building footprints, elevation, and population density operate at different spatial scales and require simultaneous querying during agent decision‑making. The “drag‑and‑drop” paradigm, while accessible, sacrifices computational efficiency and customisability, particularly for memory‑intensive operations like temporal interpolation of climate data across thousands of simulation ticks \citep{2021.DG.DMI,2013.AD.GMA}. These constraints necessitate custom solutions when modelling regional‑scale vector populations with high spatiotemporal resolution.

\subsection{High‑Performance Computing Approaches in Multi‑Agent Systems}
Recent advances in MAS for ecological applications have increasingly leveraged High‑Performance Computing (HPC) architectures to overcome scalability limitations. Contemporary frameworks emphasise concurrent data structures and spatial indexing algorithms that enable efficient simulation of millions of interacting agents across distributed computing environments. The spatial indexing challenge—rapidly identifying neighbours within an agent’s perception radius—has driven innovation in grid‑based partitioning, quad‑trees, and R‑tree implementations optimised for parallel access patterns \citep{2021.DG.DMI}. Thread‑safety mechanisms, particularly Java’s `StampedLock` with optimistic read patterns, have demonstrated order‑of‑magnitude improvements over traditional synchronisation methods by allowing concurrent read operations during intensive spatial queries while maintaining data consistency for position updates. These technical considerations justify the development of custom simulation engines over pre‑packaged solutions: while commercial and open‑source platforms provide rapid prototyping capabilities, they often lack the low‑level control required for memory‑mapped raster access, lock‑free concurrent data structures, and cache‑aware spatial indexing necessary for large‑scale simulations. The present framework addresses these gaps through a Java‑based architecture that balances object‑oriented design with HPC optimisations, enabling simulation of ≥500,000 agents with integrated environmental layers while maintaining sub‑second tick times—performance characteristics unattainable with general‑purpose ABM platforms \citep{2024.RR.COE}.

\section{Materials and Methods}

We developed a novel, high‑performance agent‑based modelling framework to simulate urban mosquito population dynamics. The system integrates heterogeneous environmental datasets with behavioural rules to model mosquito life cycles, breeding site utilisation, and human‑vector interactions at fine spatiotemporal resolutions.

\subsection{Framework Architecture and Computational Design}
The MAS system was implemented as an object‑oriented Java framework designed specifically for the computational demands of regional‑scale vector modelling. The architecture was built around several core design principles: modularity for integrating diverse data sources, performance optimisation for handling large agent populations, and flexibility for adapting to different mosquito species and environmental contexts (see Figure \ref{fig:arch}).

\subsubsection{Layered Information Model}
The environmental representation follows a stacked‑layer paradigm where heterogeneous data sources are integrated into a unified spatial framework. Static environmental variables are managed through specialised raster layer management classes. These classes implement efficient coordinate‑to‑value mapping using georeferenced bounding boxes and bilinear interpolation, enabling agents to query local environmental conditions during simulation runtime with minimal computational overhead. Specifically, for memory‑intensive datasets exceeding 100 MB, we implemented the `MemoryMappedRasterLayer` class which uses Java’s NIO memory‑mapped file channels, allowing direct access to disk‑based raster data without loading entire datasets into heap memory. This approach reduces memory footprint by approximately 70 \% for large‑scale simulations while maintaining sub‑millisecond access times for environmental queries.

Spatiotemporal climate variables in NetCDF format require specialised handling due to their time‑varying nature. The `InterpolatedRasterLayer` class implements a three‑dimensional data structure [time][latitude][longitude] with linear temporal interpolation between hourly climate reanalysis timesteps. The class maintains an in‑memory cache of interpolated values for the current simulation time, using a `ReentrantReadWriteLock` synchronisation mechanism that allows concurrent read access by thousands of agents while ensuring thread‑safe updates during temporal transitions. This design enables smooth diurnal temperature cycles and precipitation events to influence agent behaviour without computational bottlenecks.

\subsubsection{Agent Representation and Management}
The framework distinguishes between two fundamental agent classes based on mobility and autonomy. The `LivingAgent` class is designed for entities with comprehensive internal state management—in our case, mosquitoes—including: (1) developmental stage (egg, larva, pupa, adult) with stage‑specific physiological parameters, (2) energy reserves (0.0–1.0 scale) depleted by activity and replenished through feeding, (3) age (in 15‑minute simulation ticks) tracking cumulative exposure, (4) gravidity status indicating reproductive readiness, and (5) behavioural state (resting, active, seeking). These state variables are implemented using atomic data types (`AtomicBoolean`, `AtomicInteger`, `AtomicReference`) to ensure thread‑safe concurrent modification during parallel rule evaluation.

The `InertAgent` class is designed for stationary entities that maintain an evolving internal state throughout the simulation. These agents represent static environmental features (such as water containers or breeding sites) that interact dynamically with living agents at runtime. Although they lack autonomous movement, they remain active participants in the ecosystem’s logic. Agent organisation follows a hierarchical structure where agents are grouped into layers managed by the `AgentLayer` class. Each agent layer hosts a homogeneous population of either living or inert agents. This separation allows for differentiated rule sets and update frequencies optimised for each agent type. The `AgentContainer` class within each layer implements a partitioned storage system that distributes agents across multiple thread‑safe queues, enabling parallel processing during simulation ticks with minimal inter‑thread contention.

\subsubsection{Performance Optimisation}
Simulating urban‑scale mosquito populations (≥500,000 agents) necessitated several computational optimisations. We designed a `SpatialRegistry` class that implements a grid‑based spatial indexing system with 0.001‑degree resolution (∼111 m cells), providing O(1) access time for agent position updates and neighbour queries. We employed Java’s `StampedLock` synchronisation primitive which supports optimistic read locking, allowing concurrent read operations during the intensive neighbour‑finding phase of agent rule evaluation while maintaining data consistency for position updates.

To address the high object turnover inherent in mosquito population dynamics (daily birth/death rates ∼10 \%), we implemented an object pooling system through the `AgentLifecycleManager` class. This manager maintains recyclable agent instances in size‑limited pools (maximum 10,000 agents per type), significantly reducing garbage collection pauses by reinitialising rather than reallocating agent objects. During simulation execution, birth events draw from these pools (cache hit ∼85 \%), while deceased agents are returned for future reuse rather than being garbage collected.

Batch processing further enhances performance by aggregating lifecycle events (births, deaths, movements) into bulk operations. The system accumulates individual events throughout the rule evaluation phase, then processes them collectively using optimised bulk operations in the `SpatialRegistry`, reducing synchronisation overhead by up to 90 \% compared to individual event processing.

\subsection{Rule‑Based Logic and Decision Engine}
Agent decision‑making follows a modular rule‑based paradigm implemented in the `RuleEngine` class. Each rule consists of three components: (1) a condition expressed in JavaScript syntax, (2) an action identifier from a predefined set of behavioural primitives, and (3) a priority integer determining evaluation order when multiple conditions are satisfied. The rule engine utilises the GraalVM polyglot runtime to evaluate conditions within isolated JavaScript contexts, providing sandboxed execution while maintaining high performance (∼100,000 rule evaluations/second/core).

Rules are parameterised through a token system that maps environmental layer names to accessible variables within condition expressions. For example, a temperature raster layer named “t2m” can be accessed via the token “temperature” in rule conditions. This abstraction layer decouples rule logic from underlying data structures, allowing easy rule modifications and human readability. The engine maintains a thread‑local cache of compiled rule conditions, reducing parsing overhead for frequently evaluated rules by approximately 95 \%.

The action repertoire includes biologically grounded behaviours: “die” (agent mortality), “get\_gravid” (reproductive maturation), “lay\_eggs” (oviposition in suitable containers), “hatch” (egg‑to‑larva transition), “feed” (energy acquisition), “move\_random” (dispersal), “reproduce” (sexual reproduction), “pupate” (larva‑to‑pupa metamorphosis), “emerge” (pupa‑to‑adult eclosion), “dry\_out” (container desiccation), “freeze” (cold‑induced mortality), “evaporate” (water loss), “rest”/“stop\_resting” (behavioural state transitions), and “rest\_in\_building” (anthropogenic shelter seeking). Each action modifies agent state variables and/or triggers spatial registry updates, with critical actions (e.g., “die”, “lay\_eggs”) automatically invalidating spatial caches to maintain data consistency.

Rule priorities range from 1 (lowest) to 10 (highest), with higher‑priority rules evaluated first. This hierarchy ensures that critical behaviours (e.g., mortality from extreme temperatures at priority 10) supersede less urgent actions (e.g., movement at priority 2). Within each priority level, rules are evaluated sequentially until a terminal action (e.g., “die”) executes, at which point further rule evaluation ceases for that agent during the current tick, optimising computational resources.

\subsection{Case Study Design: \textit{Anopheles stephensi} in Dire Dawa, Ethiopia}
\subsubsection{Study Area and Justification}
We selected Dire Dawa, Ethiopia (9.604° N, 41.856° E) as our validation case study due to several factors: (1) confirmed presence of invasive \textit{Anopheles stephensi} based on recent entomological surveys \citep{2020.MB.GDO,2021.MB.AUO,2022.AA.VCS}, (2) representative East African urban ecology with mixed formal/informal settlements, (3) available high‑resolution environmental datasets, and (4) epidemiological significance as an emerging malaria transmission zone. \textit{Anopheles stephensi} presents particular modelling challenges due to its behavioural plasticity; unlike most African malaria vectors, it thrives in urban environments by exploiting anthropogenic water containers for breeding and exhibits both endophilic (indoor‑resting) and exophilic (outdoor‑resting) behaviours \citep{2020.MS.ANM}.

The simulation domain encompassed a 5‑km radius circular buffer (≈78.5 km²) centred on Dire Dawa’s urban centre. This spatial extent captures the typical flight range of \textit{Anopheles stephensi} (usually <1 km \citep{1966.MA.FRL}) while including peri‑urban transition zones where breeding site availability changes dramatically. The buffer approach also accommodated spatial uncertainty in agent initialisation and prevented edge effects from influencing population dynamics.

\subsubsection{Data Integration}
Environmental data integration followed a multi‑source, multi‑resolution approach. Elevation data (30 m resolution) originated from NASA’s Shuttle Radar Topography Mission (SRTM), resampled to 100 m resolution using cubic convolution to balance detail and computational load. This data was accessed via the Google Earth Engine API. Building footprints were derived from the Google Earth Engine building footprint dataset, with density calculated using 100 m grid cells to represent urbanisation gradients. Population distribution used WorldPop’s 100 m resolution constrained‑adjusted estimates for 2020, providing realistic human host availability.

Historical climate time series were derived from ERA5‑Land reanalysis data at 0.1° spatial resolution and hourly temporal resolution. We extracted a 4‑month period (September–December 2025) encompassing the post‑rainy season when \textit{Anopheles stephensi} populations typically peak \citep{2023.AK.DAA}. The dataset included 2 m temperature (K), total precipitation (m), and derived variables including relative humidity and dew point temperature. These hourly values were temporally interpolated to 15‑minute simulation ticks using the `InterpolatedRasterLayer` class’s linear interpolation, capturing diurnal temperature fluctuations critical for mosquito physiology.

At the time of writing, concurrent field entomological data for Dire Dawa are not available to the authors. The evaluation protocol described in Section 4.5 anticipates that future work will incorporate:

    \item Species identification (morphology or PCR) to confirm \textit{An. stephensi} presence.

\subsection{Simulation Parameters}
A 60‑day simulation (September 1–October 30, 2025) was initialised with parameter values derived from published \textit{Anopheles stephensi} ecology (Tables \ref{tab:agent_rules} and \ref{tab:model_parameters}). Living agents were spatially distributed using a weighted random placement algorithm favouring areas with: (1) building density >20 \%, (2) population density >50 persons/km², and (3) elevation between 1,000–1,500 m. This produced an initial population of 500,000 mosquitoes with stage distribution: 60 \% adults, 25 \% larvae, 10 \% pupae, 5 \% eggs, reflecting typical age structure during seasonal peaks \citep{2020.MS.ANM,2020.MB.GDO,2021.FG.ASM}.

Inert agents (water containers) were distributed according to a Poisson point process with intensity varying by building density: high‑density urban areas (≥40 \% building coverage) received 4 containers/100 m², medium‑density (20–40 \%) received 2 containers/100 m², and low‑density (<20 \%) received 1 container/100 m². Each container was initialised with randomised attributes: water volume (30–100 \% capacity), egg count (0–50), larval count (0–20), and capacity (100–500 individuals), representing heterogeneous container types from small jars to large water tanks.

\subsection[PLACEHOLDER: Planned Evaluation Protocol Following TRACE/ODD]{PLACEHOLDER: Planned Evaluation Protocol Following TRACE and ODD Standards}
To ensure the framework’s biological fidelity, predictive power, and utility for public health decision‑making, we have designed a multi‑step evaluation protocol that will be executed as field data become available. The protocol follows the TRACE (Transparent and Comprehensive Ecological Modelling) documentation standard and the ODD (Overview, Design concepts, Details) protocol for agent‑based models. Below we outline the eight steps; where data are not yet available, we indicate placeholders.

\subsubsection{Step 1: Data Compilation and Pre‑processing}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Field data collection [PLACEHOLDER]}: Weekly adult/larval counts from sentinel sites; species confirmation (morphology/PCR); in‑situ temperature, rainfall; GPS of water tanks.
    \item \textbf{Remote sensing data}: ERA5‑Land (already integrated); SRTM elevation; WorldPop; building footprints (OSM or satellite).
    \item \textbf{Data alignment}: Resample all rasters to common grid (0.001°); interpolate time series to 15‑minute ticks.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Step 2: Parameterisation and Calibration}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Life‑cycle parameters}: Literature values for \textit{An. stephensi} (already implemented). If local laboratory data become available, coefficients will be refined using Bayesian inference or least‑squares fitting.
    \item \textbf{Behavioural rule parameters}: Movement step calibrated from mark‑release‑recapture data [PLACEHOLDER]; resting behaviour from indoor resting density surveys [PLACEHOLDER]; host‑seeking thresholds from observed biting rates.
    \item \textbf{Calibration procedure}: Run simulation for the period of field data (e.g., 3–6 months). Use Approximate Bayesian Computation (ABC) or grid search to adjust hatching probability multiplier, tank drying rates, and adult mortality baseline. Objective: minimise RMSE between simulated and observed adult abundance.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Step 3: Internal Validation (Structural and Face)}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Track individual mosquitoes through stages; verify mean stage durations at given temperature match literature values.
    \item Compute stage transition probabilities from simulation and compare with Metzler matrix predictions (chi‑square test).
    \item Spatial consistency: simulated adult mosquitoes should cluster near water tanks (Moran’s I); average distance to nearest tank < flight range (<500 m).
    \item Energy and resting logic: ensure that mosquitoes that never rest die from exhaustion; resting individuals have higher survival.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Step 4: Quantitative External Validation}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Temporal validation [PLACEHOLDER]}: Pearson/Spearman correlation between weekly simulated adult abundance and field trap counts (acceptable threshold r > 0.6). Additional metrics: RMSE, normalised RMSE, bias.
    \item \textbf{Spatial validation [PLACEHOLDER]}: Overlay simulated adult density with trap locations; calculate area under ROC curve (AUC) for presence/absence above a threshold; Mantel test or matrix correlation.
    \item \textbf{Event validation [PLACEHOLDER]}: Compare predicted week of maximum adult abundance with observed peak; define outbreak threshold (e.g., >50 adults/trap night) and compute sensitivity/specificity.
    \item \textbf{Hindcasting [PLACEHOLDER]}: Run simulation for a past period not used in calibration (e.g., another year) and compare with independent data.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Step 5: Sensitivity and Uncertainty Analysis}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{One‑at‑a‑time (OAT) sensitivity}: Vary each species parameter ±20 \% and measure change in peak adult abundance, time to peak, total mosquito‑days.
    \item \textbf{Global sensitivity (Sobol indices)}: Latin Hypercube sampling (n=1000) of all parameters simultaneously; compute first‑order and total‑order Sobol indices.
    \item \textbf{Uncertainty propagation}: Run 100 ensemble simulations with parameter sets drawn from literature uncertainty ranges (± SD). Report prediction intervals (5th–95th percentiles) for adult abundance.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Step 6: Scenario Analysis and Surveillance Utility}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{What‑if scenarios}: Simulate indoor residual spraying (IRS) or larviciding (reduce adult survival or tank larval survival by a percentage); climate change (+2 °C, altered precipitation); urbanisation (increase building/population density).
    \item \textbf{Risk mapping}: Produce high‑resolution (100 m) risk maps of adult mosquito abundance and tank occupancy.
    \item \textbf{Early warning thresholds}: Identify meteorological triggers (e.g., three consecutive days with temperature >25 °C and rainfall >10 mm) that precede a simulated outbreak by two weeks; propose a decision‑support tool.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Step 7: Reproducibility and Reporting}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Package simulation code, YAML configuration, input rasters, and analysis notebooks in a public repository (Zenodo/GitHub). Include a Dockerfile or Conda environment.
    \item Provide full ODD protocol and TRACE documentation.
    \item Benchmark against a simpler model (e.g., degree‑day model without spatial component) to demonstrate added value of the agent‑based spatial approach.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Step 8: Publication and Communication}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Target journals: \textit{Ecological Modelling}, \textit{PLOS Neglected Tropical Diseases}, \textit{Malaria Journal}, \textit{Environmental Modelling \& Software}.
    \item Key figures: study area map with tank/trap locations; time series of simulated vs observed abundance (with uncertainty bands); spatial risk map; sensitivity tornado plot; scenario comparison.
    \item Policy brief: translate findings into actionable recommendations for Dire Dawa vector control (when to concentrate larviciding, which meteorological indices predict high risk).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Spatio‑Temporal Population Dynamics}
The framework successfully synthesised the complex lifecycle dynamics of 340,126 total mosquito agents over 60 days. Initial population states (Day 1.04) showed a robust distribution: 152,081 Adults (61 \%), 60,768 Larvae (24 \%), and 36,553 Pupae (15 \%).

Unlike smaller cohorts, this high‑density population exhibited a distinct “resilience phase” before environmental attrition. A precipitous decline was observed as the modelled 2 m temperature rose from 26.1 °C to a peak of 37.1 °C by Day 8.3. By Day 26, the population had stabilised at 5,487 agents, representing a survival rate of 2.2 \% of the peak population. This collapse confirms the model’s sensitivity to cumulative thermal stress, accurately reflecting the biological limits of the species.

\subsection{Urban Micro‑Refugia and Spatial Clustering}
Spatial analysis revealed that the vector‑infrastructure interface is the primary determinant of persistence. Using k‑d tree proximity analysis, the mean distance of adult mosquitoes to the nearest water tank remained remarkably constant between 44.5 and 46.2 m, despite massive population flux. As environmental temperatures increased, the spatial density contracted into “hotspots” directly over water tank coordinates. This confirms that \textit{An. stephensi} can persist in arid urban environments provided that human‑made water storage infrastructure is available within a 50 m flight radius.

\subsection{Thermal Constraints and Resource Scarcity}
The simulation statistics reveal the intense computational and biological interaction between vector agents and the urban landscape. The final statistics indicated a total “rules evaluated” count of 58,830 per tick for the water tank layer. This high volume of interaction suggests that despite the vast urban space, mosquito agents consistently localised and interacted with the 9,805 identified resource points.

The absence of active mosquito agents in the final simulation snapshot (Tick 5760) points to a complete population collapse driven by the environmental “tipping point.” This collapse was precipitated by a sustained thermal breach; the modelled 2 m temperature exceeded 35.0 °C mid‑simulation (reaching a peak of 37.1 °C), which surpassed the survival threshold for all life stages. This result highlights the framework’s utility in identifying specific environmental boundaries where climatic variables override the presence of favourable urban infrastructure, effectively defining the “thermal niche” of \textit{An. stephensi} in high‑altitude or arid regions.

\subsection{Computational Scalability and Framework Performance}
To evaluate the framework’s utility for large‑scale urban surveillance, we quantified the computational overhead required to simulate the high‑density \textit{An. stephensi} population. The simulation successfully processed a total of 340,126 mosquito agent life‑cycles alongside 9,805 static water infrastructure agents over a 60‑day temporal window (5,760 ticks).

Despite the high agent density, the system maintained high performance with an average tick time of 26.75 ms. The total runtime of 612.85 seconds (approximately 10 minutes) on standard hardware (Memory Total: 1950 MB) demonstrates that this rule‑based MAS framework is viable for deployment in resource‑constrained environments where high‑performance computing clusters are unavailable.

The model’s biological fidelity was maintained through the execution of 22 distinct behaviour rules for mosquito agents and 6 for water infrastructure. During the simulation, the framework effectively managed the transition of agents through life stages until environmental thresholds were reached. The “Inert” status of the water tank agents allowed a high volume of spatial queries (averaging 58,830 rule evaluations per tick) without degrading system performance, facilitating a high‑resolution analysis of the vector‑resource interface.

The spatial registry used a fixed search radius of approximately 55.7 m, mirroring the biological flight range and sensory distance of \textit{Anopheles} species in urban settings. This parameter ensured that mosquito‑water interactions were localised and biologically realistic, leading to the observed clustering patterns around urban containers. The framework optimised thread usage via a `SimulationEngine` with a core pool size of 3, ensuring that even at peak agent density (250,000+ agents), the system avoided memory leaks and maintained a stable thread state (Uptime: 10.12 minutes). \section{Discussion}

The findings of this study demonstrate that a layered, rule‑based Multi‑Agent Simulation provides a sophisticated lens through which to view the invasion dynamics of \textit{Anopheles stephensi} in urban African landscapes. By bridging high‑resolution GIS data with autonomous agent logic, the framework moves beyond the limitations of traditional compartmental models (e.g., SIR or SEIR), which typically assume uniform mixing and spatial homogeneity. In our simulation, the emergence of localised “micro‑habitats”—where agents remained clustered around water tanks despite a regional temperature‑driven population crash (Section 5)—underscores the critical importance of micro‑spatial granularity. This biological‑technical synergy suggests that \textit{An. stephensi} does not persist as a broad regional population but as a series of highly localised clusters tied to urban infrastructure. For public health authorities in cities like Addis Ababa, this implies that surveillance should not be uniform; instead, a “precision targeting” approach within a 50 m radius of water storage points is likely to be more effective and resource‑efficient.

A primary contribution of this research is its ability to address the “data gap” in resource‑constrained regions through the generation of high‑fidelity synthetic data. Vector‑borne disease surveillance in Africa often suffers from a lack of longitudinal field data \citep{2020.MS.ANM}. Our results demonstrate that even when based on fundamental ecological rules, the framework synthesises population trends that align with established ecological theory, such as the high‑altitude thermal barrier. The observation that a peak temperature of 37.1 °C triggered a population collapse identifies a specific climatic “tipping point” for \textit{An. stephensi} in the Ethiopian highlands. For policy‑makers, this synthetic output serves as a predictive baseline, allowing them to identify potential “hotspots” where the vector might persist during warmer seasons or following rapid changes in urban infrastructure. By simulating these dynamics in silico, we provide a decision‑support tool that can guide field teams to vulnerable areas before an outbreak occurs.

From a computational perspective, the performance metrics observed in Section 5 validate the architectural choices of object pooling and concurrent spatial indexing using `StampedLock` optimisations. The ability to process over 1.27 million agent‑rule evaluations per second while maintaining an average tick time of 26.75 ms for a population of 250,000+ agents is significant. It confirms that high‑resolution modelling of entire metropolitan areas is computationally feasible on standard research hardware (Memory Total: 1950 MB), a prerequisite for deploying such tools in regions where access to HPC clusters is limited. Furthermore, the decoupled nature of the rule engine provides “plug‑and‑play” adaptability. This architecture is future‑proofed; the same engine could be pivoted to model other neglected tropical diseases (NTDs), such as visceral leishmaniasis, simply by swapping agent‑rule definitions for sandflies, without necessitating a redesign of the core spatial or temporal registry.

Despite these strengths, the simulation is not without limitations that warrant further empirical calibration. The rapid population crash observed in the results highlights the high sensitivity of our rule engine to temperature, which may be modulated in the real world by mosquitoes seeking refuge in indoor environments or cooler micro‑climates not fully captured in the 2 m temperature raster. Furthermore, while the current 5 km radius covers a substantial urban‑peri‑urban gradient, the anthropophilic preferences and indoor resting behaviours of \textit{An. stephensi} require more nuanced rule definitions to perfectly match Ethiopian field observations \citep{2021.FG.ASM}. The evaluation protocol described in Section 4.5 provides a clear roadmap to address these limitations: as soon as field data become available (abundance time series, spatial trap catches, container surveys), we will perform rigorous external validation (Step 4), sensitivity analysis (Step 5), and scenario testing (Step 6). In particular, we will use Approximate Bayesian Computation to calibrate the most influential parameters (likely fecundity and adult mortality) and produce prediction intervals for outbreak risk.

Future work will also focus on integrating real‑time satellite climate feeds for live forecasting and implementing genetic algorithms to auto‑calibrate rule thresholds against historical outbreak data. By refining these parameters, the framework will evolve from a synthetic data generator into a live, predictive surveillance asset for vector‑borne disease management in the African context.

\section{Conclusion}

We believe this research bridges the gap between high‑performance computer science and environmental epidemiology. By developing a custom, rule‑based multi‑agent architecture, we have demonstrated that it is possible to simulate complex biological systems at scale with high spatial and temporal fidelity. The case study of \textit{Anopheles stephensi} in Dire Dawa, Ethiopia, serves as a critical proof of concept. It confirms that the framework is sensitive to real‑world environmental stressors and can be tuned to accurately model vector persistence in marginal climates. Moreover, the accompanying evaluation protocol (Section 4.5) provides a rigorous, standardised pathway to external validation, ensuring that the model can be trusted for operational use once field data are collected.

This work establishes the foundational engine required for larger‑scale continental modelling. The modularity of the rule engine ensures that the system is not limited to malaria; it is a platform that should be capable of being tuned to other vector‑borne diseases, such as neglected tropical diseases like visceral leishmaniasis. Ultimately, we believe this framework moves us closer to a “digital twin” of vector ecology, providing the synthetic data necessary to guide life‑saving public health interventions in the world’s most vulnerable regions, or regions with surveillance resource constraints.
